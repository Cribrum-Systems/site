YOLOv3 from scratch in raw PyTorch

Domain   object detection Framework   torch.nn only Result   ~55 COCO mAP@0.5 at 416 input

The system is a YOLOv3 detector implemented end to end on torch.nn primitives, with no torchvision.ops, no ultralytics, and no high-level detection framework. Every numerical primitive (IoU variants, NMS, target assignment, loss) is built in-tree, which makes the codebase auditable, easy to ship into restricted dependency environments, and fast to adapt to non-standard variations such as different anchor counts, custom classes, or alternative loss formulations. Performance matches the original paper on COCO at standard input sizes.

01Scope

YOLOv3 (Redmon & Farhadi, 2018) remains a strong real-time multi-class detector for deployments where SOTA mAP is not required but inference latency, dependency hygiene, and operational simplicity are. The system follows the published architecture and Darknet cfg/yolov3.cfg conventions exactly, with optional flags for two modern upgrades (CIoU loss, mosaic augmentation) that improve mAP at zero inference cost.

The implementation is intentionally restricted to torch.nn and torch.autograd. There is no dependence on torchvision.ops for nms or box_iou, no high-level wrappers, and no detection framework. This is a deliberate choice: it makes the code auditable line by line, simplifies deployment into restricted environments (medical, regulated, or air-gapped), and makes adapting the system to non-standard variations straightforward.

02Network architecture

The backbone is Darknet-53: 52 convolutional layers plus one fully connected head used for ImageNet pretraining. All downsampling is performed by stride-2 3×3 convolutions rather than max-pool. The five residual stages contain 1, 2, 8, 8, and 4 bottleneck-style blocks (1×1 reduce, 3×3 expand, identity shortcut). For a 416-pixel input the post-stage spatial sizes are 208, 104, 52, 26, 13.

The neck is a hand-designed FPN-style structure with two upsample-and-concat skips. The stride-32 head is built directly from the deepest backbone features, then those features are projected, upsampled, and concatenated with the stride-16 backbone tap to feed the stride-16 head, and the same pattern repeats for stride-8. Each head terminates in a 3×3 expand and a 1×1 prediction convolution emitting 3 · (5 + C) channels (three anchor priors, four box parameters, one objectness logit, and C class logits) before being reshaped into the canonical (batch, anchor, height, width, 5 + C) tensor shape.

Why this assignment

Stride-32 grid cells are large in input pixels, so they only have headroom to localise big objects; the largest priors live there. Stride-8 grid cells are dense and small, so the small priors land there. Each head therefore handles a distinct slice of the object-size distribution.

03Anchor priors

The system ships with the nine COCO anchors from the paper at 416-pixel reference, sorted ascending by area and split into three groups of three across the three heads. For non-COCO datasets the anchors are re-clustered from the training-split GT boxes using k-means with IoU distance rather than Euclidean distance on (width, height). Euclidean has scale variance that lets large boxes dominate the clustering loss; the IoU-based formulation reduces to a closed-form intersection of two origin-centered rectangles and avoids that pathology.

The diagnostic for any anchor set is the mean IoU between every training box and its assigned cluster; the published paper reports approximately 67% on COCO with k=9. Anchors must be clustered after letterbox in input-pixel units, otherwise the clustering operates on the wrong distribution and the resulting priors miscalibrate the entire training run.

04Box parameterization

YOLOv3 predicts four raw scalars per anchor per cell. Decoding to a real box uses sigmoid on the center offsets and exponential on the size deltas. The center is constrained to lie inside the responsible grid cell (sigmoid maps the raw logit into [0, 1)); the width and height grow multiplicatively around the anchor prior (exponential gives unbounded positive scaling). Without sigmoid on the center, the network can place a center arbitrarily far from its anchor's cell, breaking the assignment invariant and producing chaotic gradients early in training.

The system tracks three coordinate spaces explicitly: the sub-cell offset space where training targets live, the grid-cell units of each head where decoded boxes are computed and IoU is evaluated against ground truth, and the input-pixel space at network input. To recover original-image coordinates, the inverse-letterbox transform is applied: subtract pad, divide by scale, then clip to the original image bounds. The order matters; reversing it shifts predictions by a sub-pixel offset that is invisible on square images and breaks evaluation on rectangular ones.

05Target assignment

For each ground-truth box, the system computes IoU between its width and height and all nine anchors treated as origin-centered rectangles. The single best-IoU anchor is the positive: the anchor whose head, scale, and grid cell are responsible for predicting that GT. All other anchors at the same cell whose IoU with the GT exceeds an ignore threshold are ignored: they contribute neither to the no-objectness loss nor to the localization or classification loss. Everything else is negative and contributes to the no-objectness term only.

The three-way split is critical. Without an ignore band, near-correct predictions at unassigned anchors are pushed down by the no-objectness loss while the correct prediction gets pulled up by the localization loss, producing fighting gradients at exactly the same visual location. The ignore mask resolves the conflict cleanly.

Original YOLOv3 enforces one positive per ground-truth box; modern variants (YOLOv5+) relax this with multi-anchor or multi-cell matching to densify supervision. The system follows the original convention with a flag to enable the relaxed variant when training data is sparse.

06Loss design

The total loss combines three streams: a localization term over positive anchors only, an objectness term split into pos and neg streams with separate weights, and a per-class BCE-with-logits classification term over positive anchors only. Per-class BCE rather than softmax is the paper's key independence assumption: a single box can carry multiple labels (Person and Woman, for example), and softmax would force the model to commit to one.

The objectness imbalance is severe. A 13×13 head has 1,521 anchor cells per image, of which typically fewer than ten are positive. A naive single-mean BCE drowns the positives in negatives and converges to an all-zero objectness predictor. Splitting pos and neg streams with separate per-stream means and an explicit lambda re-weighting fixes this; the ignored-cell mask further removes the borderline cases that would otherwise produce fighting gradients.

Two regression formulations

Paper-faithful: MSE on the four box parameters with a (2 − w·h) per-box scale factor that up-weights small boxes, matching the original Darknet C implementation.

Modern (default): Complete-IoU loss on decoded boxes. CIoU adds a center-distance penalty normalized by the enclosing-box diagonal and an aspect-ratio consistency term to the standard IoU. The paper-prescribed detail is to detach the aspect-ratio scaling from autograd so gradient flows through the geometric terms only. CIoU is scale-aware and gains 1 to 2 mAP over MSE on COCO at zero inference cost.

The system ships with both formulations behind a flag. Default is CIoU. The MSE path is retained for exact paper reproduction and as a debugging baseline.

07Non-maximum suppression

Three NMS variants are provided and selectable per deployment:

  • Per-class greedy NMS. The COCO standard. Two distinct objects of different classes can legitimately overlap (a person riding a horse, a cup on a desk), and per-class NMS preserves both correctly.
  • Class-agnostic greedy NMS. Preferred when downstream consumers treat all detections symmetrically (crowd counting, generic foreground masking).
  • Soft-NMS (Bodla et al., 2017) with linear or Gaussian decay. Decays scores rather than zeroing them; the Gaussian variant is parameter-light and gains roughly +1 to 1.3 mAP on COCO over hard NMS without retraining.

All three operate in input-pixel space after the inverse-letterbox transform and are implemented with vectorised IoU rather than nested loops; the only Python-level loop iterates one survivor at a time, computing IoU against the entire remaining tail in a single tensor op. At typical pre-NMS counts of a few hundred boxes per image after confidence filtering, this scales fine.

08Modern upgrades

Two modern additions are available behind flags, both replacing original choices with drop-in upgrades that have no inference-time cost.

CIoU loss replaces MSE-on-box-parameters as the default localization term. It adds a center-distance penalty and an aspect-ratio consistency term to standard IoU, is scale-aware via the enclosing-box diagonal normalization, and is the single highest-leverage improvement over the paper. Reliable +1 to 2 mAP on COCO.

Mosaic augmentation (introduced in YOLOv4) samples four images, places each into a quadrant of a canvas of double the input size with random crop placement, and resizes the canvas back to the input size for training. The augmentation transforms ground truth alongside the image and drops boxes that shrink below a minimum area. Mosaic especially benefits the stride-8 head's small-object recall, since the four-way crop puts many small instances at varied scales into a single training sample.

The training schedule itself follows cfg/yolov3.cfg: SGD with momentum 0.9 and weight decay 5e-4, base learning rate 1e-3, batch 64, polynomial-power-4 burn-in for the first 1,000 iterations, step decay at 400 k and 450 k, total 500 k iterations, with optional cosine decay as a modern alternative. Multi-scale training resamples the input resolution to a random multiple of 32 in [320, 608] every 10 batches, which produces a model robust across the inference resolutions that real deployments actually use.

09Operational characteristics

With ImageNet-pretrained Darknet-53 weights, COCO mAP@0.5 lands at approximately 55 at 416-pixel input and 57 to 58 at 608-pixel input, matching the original paper. Without pretraining, the model still trains from random initialization but adds roughly 30 to 50 epochs of convergence time and typically loses 2 to 3 mAP. Pretraining the Darknet-53 backbone as an ImageNet classifier first, then loading those weights into the detector with the head and neck initialised from random, is the standard recipe and is supported out of the box.

The whole codebase is intentionally compact, roughly 500 lines of torch.nn, and depends only on the standard PyTorch + NumPy + OpenCV stack for image IO, letterbox, and Poisson-blend operations. There is no detection-specific dependency anywhere in the tree, which makes the system straightforward to deploy into restricted environments and easy to adapt for non-standard class counts, alternative anchor sets, or single-class single-shot variants.

Summary

YOLOv3 is a clean compositional artifact: a fully convolutional backbone, a hand-designed FPN-style neck with two upsample-and-concat skips, three prediction heads, anchor priors clustered with IoU distance, decoded through sigmoid-on-center and exponential-on-size, supervised by a three-stream loss with a tri-state assignment mask, and post-processed by greedy per-class NMS. The system delivers each of these components on torch.nn primitives only, with optional CIoU and mosaic upgrades, and matches the original paper's COCO numbers at standard input sizes.
