CRNN OCR for Latin and Chinese

Domain   line-text OCR, joint Latin + Simplified Chinese Hardware   single RTX 5070 Ti Vocabulary   ~7,100 classes

The system is a production-grade joint Latin and Simplified Chinese line-text OCR built on the CRNN+CTC stack at a roughly 7,100-class joint vocabulary. It handles Latin-only, Chinese-only, and mixed-script content ("iPhone 14 Pro", "Wi-Fi 密码", "5号楼B座") and is trained on a single RTX 5070 Ti in roughly two weeks of total project time, including data pipeline, charset curation, pretraining, and fine-tuning on RCTW-17, LSVT, and ReCTS. CRNN+CTC remains the right baseline for alignment-free monotonic line recognition in 2026 even with transformers everywhere, because the architecture maps cleanly onto the problem and runs in O(T · U) with no autoregressive decode.

01Scope and the Chinese problem

Latin OCR is comparatively easy at this scale: a 95-class softmax, MJSynth-style synthetic data, and a day of pretraining are enough for production-quality results. Chinese turns three knobs hard simultaneously and the architecture has to absorb all three at once.

  1. Vocabulary explosion. A useful production vocabulary is roughly 7,100 classes versus 95 for Latin, two orders of magnitude larger.
  2. Font scarcity. Latin OCR pipelines (SynthText, MJSynth) draw from ~1,400 fonts. CJK has perhaps 10 to 20 high-quality fonts with full coverage of the target vocabulary, an order of magnitude fewer.
  3. Heavy frequency tail. The top 1,000 Chinese characters cover >95% of token mass; the long tail is genuinely hard to train without targeted data construction.

The architecture changes very little to handle Chinese; the data pipeline and synthesis strategy carry the load. The system therefore over-invests in synthetic data, charset design, and font coverage validation, and uses a deliberately simple recogniser.

02Architecture

The model is the canonical Shi-Bai-Yao 2015 CRNN: a 7-layer VGG-style convolutional backbone, a map-to-sequence reshape that squeezes the height to 1, two stacked bidirectional LSTMs, and a CTC transcription head. The architecture is unchanged from the original publication; what is tuned is the input height and the vertical pooling cascade.

The two non-square pooling layers (1×2 windows) are the key trick. They preserve horizontal resolution while continuing to halve vertical resolution, so a 100-pixel word produces ~25 frames rather than the ~6 you would get from four square pooling layers. Each frame's effective receptive-field width is roughly 1.5 character widths with a frame stride of 4 pixels, which means adjacent frames overlap heavily and each frame sees its character plus context. That overlap is what lets the BiLSTM disambiguate split glyphs (lowercase L versus the left half of H, the digram rn versus m) without committing to character boundaries.

Input height for CJK

The system bumps input height from the Latin-default 32 pixels to 48 pixels. A typical Chinese character has 10 to 20 strokes packed into a square; at 32 pixels after stride-32 vertical downsampling, single strokes become sub-pixel-distinguishable, which is the known failure mode behind misreads like 入 vs 人, 千 vs 干, and 末 vs 未. The pooling cascade is adjusted to a 3-2-2-2-2 pattern that produces 48 to 24 to 12 to 6 to 3 to 1, with a final 3×1 pool collapsing the last height dimension. Naively upsampling the input to 48 while keeping 32-tuned strides does not work and is a common implementation trap.

03CTC, briefly

Connectionist Temporal Classification (Graves et al., 2006) is what makes CRNN work. The model emits one softmax distribution per frame over the joint vocabulary plus a special blank symbol; CTC computes the marginal probability of a target label sequence by summing over all alignments via a forward-backward dynamic program in O(T · U) time. The loss is the negative log of that marginal.

The defining mathematical property of CTC is that the gradient with respect to the pre-softmax logits reduces to softmax outputs minus a soft target distribution, where the soft target is the CTC posterior computed by forward-backward. This is structurally identical to ordinary cross-entropy except the target is the marginalised alignment posterior rather than a one-hot label. The practical consequence is that CTC drops into any softmax network as a pure loss-function replacement, with no special numerical handling needed at the gradient interface and full compatibility with bf16 autocast everywhere except the loss itself.

Three real consequences of CTC's frame-wise conditional independence shape the system:

  • No internal language model. Output-side linguistic regularities come only via the encoder's representation of the image. Plain CTC under-models language and benefits substantially from an external n-gram or neural LM at decode time, integrated via prefix beam search.
  • Peaky alignments. CTC concentrates almost all probability mass on a single dominant alignment with most frames emitting blank; this is well-documented and is not a bug.
  • Modular LM integration. The flip side of having no internal LM is that external LMs slot into prefix beam search cleanly without retraining, which attention encoder-decoders cannot do.

For Chinese, an external LM helps less than for English because character-level n-gram coverage of CJK is weaker and Chinese line text in scenes is often non-grammatical short tokens (signs, codes, addresses). The system uses greedy CTC decoding by default and supports prefix beam search with optional LM integration where the deployment justifies it.

04Vocabulary design

The output vocabulary is the single highest-impact design decision for production Chinese OCR. The system uses Tier 1 plus Tier 2 of the 通用规范汉字表 (Table of General Standard Chinese Characters, 2013): 6,500 characters covering >99.9% of contemporary mainland Chinese text by frequency, plus 62 Latin alphanumerics, ASCII punctuation, fullwidth Chinese punctuation, and roughly 500 frequent Tier 3 / domain characters, for a total of approximately 7,113 classes including the CTC blank.

Going to GBK scale (~21,000 characters) is wasteful: tail classes are untrainable without billions of samples and bloat the output projection. Going below 3,500 misses common characters in real-world domain text such as menus, addresses, and signage. Tier 1+2 is the correct production setpoint.

Halfwidth and fullwidth punctuation are different classes

The half-width comma , (U+002C) and the fullwidth comma ， (U+FF0C) are different code points with different visual metrics. Real signage uses fullwidth in Chinese-only contexts and halfwidth in mixed contexts, and the distinction is recoverable from glyph advance width. The system keeps them as separate classes throughout and only normalises them at evaluation time, matching ICDAR competition protocols.

Radical-based decomposition (Ideographic Description Sequences) is deliberately not used. Decomposing each character into roughly 500 components plus 12 IDS structural operators collapses the output vocabulary to ~512 and is research-grade for zero-shot handwritten character recognition, but it has serious downsides for production line OCR: many-to-many mapping (multiple decompositions exist), 3 to 7× sequence inflation forcing wider input or denser strides, severe class imbalance among radicals, and a non-trivial radical-to-character matcher at decode. Every shipped Chinese OCR (PaddleOCR, EasyOCR, Tesseract chi_sim) uses flat-character classes for these reasons. The system follows that convention.

05Synthetic data pipeline

The training corpus is dominated by synthetic data. The pipeline follows the SynthText recipe (Gupta, Vedaldi, Zisserman, CVPR 2016) in shape, but replaces two of its 2016 components with their 2025-era successors:

Pipeline stage Original (2016) Modern (this system)
Monocular depth Liu 2014 CNN Depth Anything V2 ViT-B
Region segmentation gPb-UCM (~30 s/image CPU) SAM 2 in automatic mode (~0.5 s/image)
Text rendering FreeType + PIL Same
Blending Poisson editing (mixed gradients) Same; OpenCV seamlessClone

The two replacements are drop-in upgrades: 30 to 100× throughput improvement at equivalent or better region quality, since SAM produces closed object-level masks that are actually better for placing text on signs, panels, and surfaces than gPb-UCM's low-level colour and texture masks ever were.

Font coverage validation is the single most damaging silent bug in CJK synthesis. Rendering a string with a font that lacks coverage for one of its characters produces a .notdef tofu box in the image while the label still says the original character, silently corrupting training data. The system pre-builds a (font, codepoint) coverage matrix at startup using fontTools and samples only fonts with full coverage of the chosen string. This is not optional.

Font selection covers the major style families: Songti (serif/print), Heiti (sans/sign), Kaiti (regular-script handwriting), Xingshu (semi-cursive), Fangsong (government documents), and Lishu (clerical/ornamental). Five to eight fonts is enough; gains plateau past ~10 because glyph-shape variance is constrained by readability.

The text-sampling regime mixes natural corpus sampling (Wikipedia, news) with uniform-vocabulary sampling so rare-but-in-vocab characters get adequate coverage despite their natural-frequency suppression. The joint Latin+Chinese mix is roughly 30% Latin-only, 50% Chinese-only with frequency plus rare-uniform oversampling, and 20% mixed-script content. Mixed is exactly where naive single-script models fail.

06Real-data fine-tuning

After synthetic pretraining the model is fine-tuned on real Chinese line-text crops from the standard public benchmarks:

Dataset Size Role
RCTW-17 12,263 images Scene text, mixed orientations.
LSVT 450,000 (50K labelled, 400K weakly labelled) Large-scale street view; the weak split is used semi-supervised.
ReCTS-25k 25,000 Signboards; high domain match for shop/storefront OCR.
ArT, ICDAR2019-MLT, MTWI ~50,000 combined Curved, multi-oriented, web text. Optional inclusion.
Synthetic Chinese String Dataset ~3.6 M Standard public synthetic; complements in-house synthesis.

The reporting metric is Character Error Rate (CER, normalised edit distance), the ICDAR-RCTW / RRC-LSVT / ReCTS standard, reported both raw and after NFKC normalisation. WER and sequence-accuracy are reported alongside for downstream consumers that need them. The system is competitive with public CJK OCR baselines on the standard line-text benchmarks at the headline metric.

07Operational characteristics

The full training stack runs on a single RTX 5070 Ti (sm_120, 16 GB) with PyTorch cu128 wheels and bf16 autocast across the conv and BiLSTM stages. The CTC loss runs in fp32 because the cuDNN CTC kernel is fp32-only; this is a deliberate dtype boundary and is the single most common implementation trap with PyTorch+CTC on modern GPUs. bf16 is preferred over fp16 because bf16 has the fp32 exponent range, no GradScaler is required, and throughput is identical on Blackwell tensor cores.

Variable-width inputs are handled by aspect-ratio bucketing: dataset indices are sorted by image width, grouped into batches, and bucket order is shuffled per epoch. For Chinese line text spanning 100 to 1,500 pixels, bucketing yields a 30 to 50% throughput improvement over global pad-to-max because almost all of the wasted padding compute disappears.

Memory at the working batch size of 128 (input height 48, varying widths) sits comfortably under 4 GB live; gradient accumulation is unnecessary on this hardware for this model. Total project time from clean repo to a working production-quality joint Latin + Chinese line OCR is roughly two weeks, dominated by data pipeline construction and font coverage curation rather than model training.

Summary

The CRNN+CTC architecture is unchanged from 2015 to 2026: the 7-conv VGG backbone, the 1×2 height-only pooling trick, the 2-layer 256-unit BiLSTM, and the CTC transcription head still define the right baseline for monotonic line recognition. What has moved underneath is the stack: cu128 wheels for sm_120, bf16 autocast on Blackwell tensor cores, and Depth Anything V2 plus SAM 2 replacing the original SynthText monocular-depth and gPb-UCM components at 30 to 100× throughput. What has not moved is the tradeoffs: CTC is still alignment-free and monotonic, scene-text recognition is still cropped-line prediction, and Chinese is still defined by font scarcity and a heavy character-frequency tail. The system is built around those constants.
